The Shape of
the Thing

On reshaping under uncertainty — and the role
of venture in the decisions that compound

Morris Clay February 2026

Every technology has a shape. Not the shape of the code or the architecture — the shape it takes when it meets the world. The packaging, the form factor, who encounters it first and what it looks like when they do. The container the thing arrives in.

I invest in deep tech at the earliest stages, and I keep noticing a mismatch. A founder shows me something genuinely brilliant — a new approach to data synchronization, a novel compute primitive, a breakthrough in how machines perceive their environment — something that makes me lean forward in my chair. And then they show me how they're planning to ship it. And I can feel it before I can articulate it: the container doesn't fit the thing inside it.

That feeling. I've been sitting with it for a while now, trying to understand what it's actually pointing at. Because I think it touches something the venture world mostly ignores. We talk endlessly about markets and teams and timing. We have frameworks for evaluating technology risk. But the actual product architecture — the decisions about what shape a technology takes when it meets the world — gets treated as an implementation detail. Something the founder figures out along the way.

It doesn't get figured out along the way. It gets negotiated — over and over, often by proxy, often without anyone naming it as the decision it actually is. A packaging choice made under time pressure. A positioning that drifts because a design partner asked for something slightly different. A form factor inherited from a demo that was never meant to be the product. Each negotiation feels small. But they compound. And undoing a shape that's already been shipped, sold against, and built around is one of the most expensive things a startup can do. There's a specific kind of exhaustion that comes from iterating on a container that was never going to fit.

the reframe

The pattern I see most often — the one that most clearly shows what shape decisions actually do — is what I think of as the reframe.

Same technology. Different story. Completely different ceiling.

A product positioned as "cheaper alternative to [incumbent]" is in a race to the bottom. You're defined by someone else's product. You compete on one axis. You attract buyers who'll leave when something cheaper appears.

Now take that same capability and frame it around a problem that didn't exist two years ago — agentic workloads that need isolation, real-time perception for autonomous systems, data governance for a world where ai touches everything. The buyer changes. The urgency changes. The willingness to pay changes. And an expansion path opens that wasn't visible before. Multiple products from the same underlying technology. None of them reachable from the "cheap X" frame.

The technology didn't change. The shape of the story changed. And the story's shape determines the company's ceiling.

This isn't a branding exercise. It's structural. When you reframe, you're changing who your first user is, how they find you, what they're willing to pay, and where the product goes next. And sometimes the same technology that struggled for years in one frame finds immediate traction in another — not because anything underneath improved, but because the container finally fit the thing inside it.

what remains

There's something larger underneath all of this that I've been turning over for months.

ai is making execution cheap. On both sides of the table. Founders prototype in an afternoon. Investors generate market maps in minutes. Code, research, content, financial models — the entire operational layer of building and investing is compressing toward zero marginal cost. Fast.

So what's left when execution is commoditized?

I used to think the answer was access — to deals, to talent, to information. But those edges are compressing too. ai can generate ten product configurations for a founding team. It can map every customer segment. It can model the financial implications of every form factor. And it's getting better at all of this, quickly.

But someone still has to choose. Someone has to sit in the room with the ambiguity — the moment when the data doesn't resolve, when the market could go two directions, when the decision is irreversible and the information is incomplete — and make the call. Not because ai can't contribute to that moment. It can, and increasingly will. But because the choosing draws on something that's hard to formalize: the accumulated weight of having been in that room many times before and having watched what happened next. Pattern recognition that lives closer to intuition than to analysis.

Taste. In the deepest sense of the word.

The founders who win in this environment aren't the ones with the best engineering — ai narrows that gap. Or the most sophisticated go-to-market — ai narrows that too. They're the ones who see the right shape before anyone else. Who look at a vast space of possible configurations and notice the one that fits.

the turn

Writing this essay did something I didn't fully expect. I thought I was writing about founders. But the lens turned around.

Investors have shapes too. We ship ourselves in containers — fund theses, value propositions, inherited activities. And I realized the thing I've spent years building — product judgment, the ability to sit with a founder and see the full landscape of possible shapes — was packaged in the standard venture container. Board seats, quarterly check-ins, portfolio support. It worked. But honestly, I could feel the same mismatch I keep diagnosing in others. The highest-leverage version of this work is concentrated at inception — the weeks when shape decisions are actually being made, before the concrete sets. Not spread thin over years.

So I reshaped around it. I work with deep tech founders at the earliest stage, intensively and for a short time, on the hardest question their company faces: what shape should this thing take? I help widen the aperture. Then the founder drives.

This essay, it turns out, is also a packaging pivot. I had to practice on myself what I keep telling founders: look at the container. Ask whether it fits. And if it doesn't — find the one that does.

The shape of the thing. It's the question that matters most now — and the one almost nobody is asking carefully enough.
