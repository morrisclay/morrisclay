The Shape of
the Thing

Why the most expensive mistake in deep tech
is one almost nobody talks about

Morris Clay February 2026

Every technology has a shape. Not the shape of the code or the architecture — the shape it takes when it meets the world. The packaging, the form factor, who encounters it first and what it looks like when they do. The container the thing arrives in.

I invest in deep tech at the earliest stages, and I keep noticing a mismatch. A founder shows me something genuinely brilliant — a new approach to data synchronization, a novel compute primitive, a breakthrough in how machines perceive their environment — something that makes me lean forward in my chair. And then they show me how they're planning to ship it. And I can feel it before I can articulate it: the container doesn't fit the thing inside it.

That feeling. I've been sitting with it for a while now, trying to understand what it's actually pointing at. Because I think it touches something the venture world mostly ignores. We talk endlessly about markets and teams and timing. We have frameworks for evaluating technology risk. But the actual product architecture — the decisions about what shape a technology takes when it meets the world — gets treated as an implementation detail. Something the founder figures out along the way.

It doesn't get figured out along the way. It gets negotiated — over and over, often by proxy, often without anyone naming it as the decision it actually is. A packaging choice made under time pressure. A positioning that drifts because a design partner asked for something slightly different. A form factor inherited from a demo that was never meant to be the product. Each negotiation feels small. But they compound. And undoing a shape that's already been shipped, sold against, and built around is one of the most expensive things a startup can do. There's a specific kind of exhaustion that comes from iterating on a container that was never going to fit.

the reframe

The pattern I see most often — the one that most clearly shows what shape decisions actually do — is what I think of as the reframe.

Same technology. Different story. Completely different ceiling.

A product positioned as "cheaper alternative to [incumbent]" is in a race to the bottom. You're defined by someone else's product. You compete on one axis. You attract buyers who'll leave the moment something cheaper shows up.

Now take that same capability and frame it around a problem that didn't exist two years ago — agentic workloads that need isolation, real-time perception for autonomous systems, data governance for a world where ai touches everything. Suddenly you're not selling against an incumbent. You're selling into urgency. The conversation changes — from "why should we switch" to "we need this and nobody else has it." And from that position, expansion paths open that were invisible from the cheap-X frame.

I've watched this pattern across the whole lifecycle. A team reframes around a new use case and suddenly closes design partners that had been stuck in evaluation for months. A company repositions before a follow-on round and the conversation with investors shifts from "interesting but early" to competitive term sheets. A founder rethinks their packaging before a key hire and lands someone who wouldn't have joined the previous version of the story. Nothing underneath changed. The container changed.

This is what makes shape decisions so easy to get wrong. The reframe isn't a branding exercise. It's structural. You're changing who finds you, what they're willing to pay, and where the product goes next. And deep tech founders feel real tension here, because they've built something genuinely better and they want the container to honor that novelty. That instinct is right — and it's also often the thing that limits them. Adoption friction is the real bottleneck. Not capability.

the gap

Here's the thing that actually bothers me.

The venture world has built real infrastructure on the demand side. Investors make introductions, open doors, connect founders to customers and capital. This is genuinely valuable work. I don't want to diminish it.

But on the supply side — the question of what exactly you're building, what form it takes, who encounters it first — founders at the frontier are mostly working through it alone. Good angels and operators contribute real thinking, but their attention is fragmented. A couple of hours here, a coffee conversation there. Nobody is going deep on the specific question of how this technology should be shaped for this market at this moment.

The obvious counterargument is accelerators. And look — they do real work. Three months of compressed learning, forced decisions, pitch practice until you can recite your tam in your sleep. Demo Day is a genuine forcing function. I'm not here to dunk on accelerators. Some of my best portfolio companies went through them.

But here's what I keep noticing: shape isn't a one-time decision. It's stage-related. The container that works for your first design partner often breaks when you're trying to close your first enterprise deal. The form factor that got you to product-market fit in one segment becomes a constraint when you try to expand. Every major transition — first paying customer, first sales hire, first platform bet — forces the question again.

Accelerators are a three-month container. They're built for inception. But the shape decisions that kill companies often happen in year two, year three — when a team has real traction and real constraints and the cost of getting it wrong has compounded. That's when you need someone in the room who has context across the full arc. Not someone meeting you for the first time, but someone who watched the first shape get chosen and understands why the new moment is different.

This is what a board seat is actually for — or could be. The relationship continuity is there. The incentive alignment is there. The question is whether investors are using that position to do this work, or defaulting to governance and quarterly check-ins.

what remains

There's a question underneath all of this that I keep circling back to — and it's not just about founders.

ai is forcing everyone to rethink their jobs. Investors are not excluded. The operational layer of venture is compressing fast. I can generate a market map in minutes that would have taken a week two years ago. Due diligence that used to require a team of analysts can be synthesized in an afternoon. The stuff that used to feel like work is becoming table stakes.

So what's left? What non-commodity value can a vc bring beyond signaling — fund brand, which is just reputation arbitrage — and capital, which is increasingly commodity?

I used to think it was access. Intros to customers, talent, other investors. But those networks are getting disintermediated too. The things I thought were hard are getting easy.

What I keep coming back to is this: someone still has to choose. Someone has to sit in the room with the ambiguity — the moment when the data doesn't resolve, when the market could go two directions, when the decision is irreversible and the information is incomplete — and help make the call. ai can inform that moment. But the choosing draws on something harder to formalize: having been in that room many times before and having watched what happened next.

Taste. That's the word I keep landing on, and I know it sounds imprecise. But I mean something specific: the ability to look at a vast space of possible configurations and notice the one that fits. Not because you can articulate why in the moment — sometimes the articulation comes later — but because you've seen enough of these decisions play out to have a feel for which shapes hold weight and which don't.

Taste isn't infallible. But it's the thing that doesn't commoditize. It compounds with experience rather than getting automated away.

The non-commodity value I can bring? Shape. Being in the room when the container decisions happen, with enough context and pattern recognition to widen the aperture. That's it.

the turn

Writing this essay did something I didn't fully expect. I thought I was writing about founders. But the lens turned around.

Investors have shapes too. We ship ourselves in containers — fund theses, value propositions, inherited activities. And I realized the thing I've spent years building — product judgment, the ability to sit with a founder and see the full landscape of possible shapes — was often packaged wrong. Not absent, but deployed at the wrong tempo. I could feel the same mismatch I keep diagnosing in others.

The work that matters isn't concentrated at inception and then done. It's being in the room when these moments happen — again and again, across years. The first shape decision. The first time that shape breaks. The pivot that isn't a pivot, just a reframe that unlocks everything. You can only be useful in those moments if you have context across the full arc, and incentive to stay engaged.

So I reshaped around it. Not a three-month program. Not quarterly check-ins. High-touch across the whole journey, with depth when the shape questions surface — which they do, reliably, at every major transition.

This essay, it turns out, is also a packaging pivot. I had to practice on myself what I keep telling founders: look at the container. Ask whether it fits. And if it doesn't — find the one that does.

The shape of the thing. It's the question that matters most now — and the one almost nobody is asking carefully enough.

This essay was written with Claude. I expect you're reading it with Claude too, or will be soon. I've made my peace with this — the same way I've made peace with not hand-rolling my own webpack configs anymore. I still write code for fun, the way some people still develop film.
